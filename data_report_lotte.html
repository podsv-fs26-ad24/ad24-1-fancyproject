Data Report — Spotify Tracks Analysis

Author

Angelina, Cécile and Charlotte

Published

April 10, 2026

Overview

This report documents the exploratory data analysis of the Spotify Tracks Dataset downloaded from Kaggle. The dataset contains 114,000 tracks across 113 genres, each described by a popularity score (0–100) and nine audio features computed by Spotify, which are: danceability, energy, valence, acousticness, instrumentalness, speechiness, liveness, loudness, and tempo. After removing duplicate tracks, the working dataset contains 89,741 unique songs.

The analysis addresses four core questions from the project charta:

  1. How is popularity distributed across songs?
  2. Which audio features correlate with popularity?
  3. How do genres differ in their audio profiles?
  4. What do the most popular songs have in common, and how does mood relate to genre?

Q1: How is popularity distributed?

Popularity scores range from 0 to 100 and follow a strongly right-skewed distribution. The median score is 33 and the mean is 33.2, but these numbers are pulled upward by a small number of high-performing tracks. The breakdown by band:

Popularity range Number of songs Share
0 (completely unheard) 9,448 10.5%
1–29 (low) 31,027 34.6%
30–69 (mid) 46,140 51.4%
70–100 (high) 3,126 3.5%

The key finding is that only 3.5% of songs score above 70, while over 10% have a score of exactly zero, which we interpret that Spotify’s algorithm registers almost no meaningful streaming activity. This extreme concentration at the low end sets up the central question of the data story: what separates the rare songs that actually reach people?

Q2: Which audio features correlate with popularity?

Between each audio feature we calculated the Pearson correlations and the popularity score. All correlations are weak. None exceeds an absolute value of 0.13, which means no single audio feature reliably predicts whether a song will be popular.

Feature Correlation (r) Direction
instrumentalness −0.127 less instrumental = more popular
loudness +0.072 louder = slightly more popular
danceability +0.064 more danceable = slightly more popular
speechiness −0.047 less speech-like = slightly more popular
acousticness −0.039 less acoustic = slightly more popular
liveness −0.014 negligible
energy +0.014 negligible
valence −0.012 negligible
tempo +0.007 negligible

The strongest signal is instrumentalness (r = −0.127): songs with vocals tend to score higher than purely instrumental tracks. Loudness and danceability follow at a distance. Energy, valence, and tempo show virtually no relationship with popularity.

The feature correlation matrix reveals stronger relationships between features than with popularity. The strongest pairs are energy with loudness (r = +0.759) and energy with acousticness (r = −0.733), meaning loud tracks tend to feel energetic, and energetic tracks tend not to be acoustic. Danceability and valence also travel together (r = +0.493): happier-sounding songs are generally easier to dance to.

Q3: How do genres differ in their audio profiles?

The dataset spans 113 genres with approximately 1,000 songs each. Six representative genres were selected for closer comparison: classical, hip-hop, jazz, metal, pop, and rock.

Genre Danceability Energy Valence Acousticness Instrumentalness Tempo
classical 0.386 0.197 0.392 0.920 0.597 108
hip-hop 0.730 0.695 0.553 0.198 0.013 118
jazz 0.489 0.309 0.487 0.766 0.109 116
metal 0.481 0.841 0.425 0.036 0.046 129
pop 0.581 0.579 0.477 0.403 0.004 121
rock 0.549 0.648 0.478 0.227 0.023 121

The contrasts are striking. Classical scores the highest acousticness (0.920) and the lowest energy (0.197) of any focus genre. Metal is the opposite: lowest acousticness (0.036), highest energy (0.841), and fastest tempo (129 BPM). Hip-hop leads on danceability (0.730) and is the least instrumental (0.013), reflecting its vocal-heavy and rhythm-driven character. Pop and rock sit in the middle on most features.

Q4: Hits vs non-hits, and the mood map

What do popular songs sound like?

Tracks were split into hits (top 10%, popularity ≥ 60, n = 9,806) and non-hits (bottom 10%, popularity = 0, n = 9,448). Comparing mean feature values:

Feature Hits Non-hits Difference
danceability 0.595 0.556 hits higher ↑
energy 0.638 0.580 hits higher ↑
loudness −7.8 dB −8.9 dB hits louder ↑
acousticness 0.279 0.388 hits lower ↓
instrumentalness 0.078 0.126 hits lower ↓
valence 0.484 0.483 negligible
tempo 120.4 118.5 negligible

Hits tend to be louder, more danceable, more energetic, less acoustic, and less instrumental than non-hits. Valence (happiness) shows almost no difference, suggesting that whether a song sounds happy or sad has little bearing on its popularity. These are patterns in the data, not a formula — correlation does not imply causation, and many non-hit songs share these same characteristics.

Mood map: valence and energy by genre

Plotting mean valence (happy and sad) against mean energy (calm and intense) places each genre in one of four mood quadrants. Of the 113 genres, 47 fall in Sad & Energetic and 46 in Happy & Energetic, meaning the vast majority of genres tend to be energetic regardless of emotional tone. Only 20 genres sit in the calm half.

Focus genre positions:

Genre Valence Energy Quadrant

Hip-hop is the only focus genre in the happy-energetic quadrant. Jazz and classical are the only two that are both calm and on the sadder side. Pop and rock sit close together, moderately energetic, slightly melancholic on average.

Summary of key findings

  • Popularity is extremely concentrated: 3.5% of songs account for the high end, 10.5% are completely unheard.
  • No single audio feature strongly predicts popularity. Instrumentalness has the highest correlation (r = −0.127), followed by loudness and danceability.
  • Genres differ dramatically in their audio profiles. Classical and metal sit at opposite extremes: pop and rock cluster in the middle.
  • Hits are louder, more danceable, and less acoustic than non-hits, but the differences are modest.
  • Most genres are energetic. The calm quadrant is dominated by classical, jazz, ambient, and sleep.
